How ADA Works

Design principles, the AI fluency framework, and evaluation

Design Approach

ADA's development follows a design-based research approach using Sandoval's (2014) conjecture mapping method. The design conjectures are testable claims about what ADA needs to do for the intended transformation to occur. Their purpose is to promote course conveners' agency and encourage meaningful dialogue around assessment and feedback design.

The Four-Phase Framework

ADA guides assessment design through four phases, informed by a collaborative decision-making flowchart grounded in four areas of educational literature: the Assessment Design Decisions Framework (Bearman et al., 2016), Evidence-Centered Assessment Design (Huff et al., 2010), Teachers' and Students' Assessment Literacy (Quilter & Gallini, 2000), and Student Partnership in Assessment (Deeley & Bovill, 2015).

1

Foundation and Purposes

Define assessment purposes (formative, summative, diagnostic, ipsative). Analyse constraints including programme context, policies, and resources. Develop mutual understanding of learner characteristics, student experiences, career aspirations, and cultural relevance.

2

Design and Alignment

Identify targeted performances and expectations (marking criteria). Unpack disciplinary core ideas, practices, and cross-cutting concepts. Design tasks, plan feedback processes, and define teacher-student and student-student interactions.

3

Validation for Validity, Fairness, and Integrity

Evaluate whether tasks enable students to demonstrate their intended learning outcomes fairly and inclusively. Assess whether the curriculum supports students, and whether feedback and grades are fit for purpose. Identify and address barriers to engagement.

4

Design and Build the Assessment

Assess effectiveness and efficiency. Consider assessment optionality for adjustments. Address how learning technologies and GenAI affect student engagement. Confirm deliverables, technology, and next steps.

The AI Fluency Framework

Underpinning all three design conjectures is the principle that human control and course conveners' meaningful agency must be actively protected, not assumed. ADA draws on Anthropic's AI Fluency Framework (Dakan and Feller, 2025), which scaffolds human-AI interaction through four dimensions.

Delegation

ADA curates evidence and surfaces institutional policies at key decision points, acts as a dialogue facilitator between stakeholders, and visualises, documents, and drafts initial outputs. These are tasks delegated to ADA so that course conveners can focus on decisions requiring human judgement.

Description

ADA is pre-populated with an institutional knowledge base containing contextual policies, local pedagogical practices, and output templates. ADA is also instructed with collaborative parameters, including dual-question prompts to promote co-creation between educators and students.

Discernment

ADA pauses and prompts human evaluation at key moments. Educator-focused and student-focused questions enable both parties to see the design from different perspectives, so that discernment becomes a structured dialogue rather than a solo act.

Diligence

ADA documents which evidence sources informed each decision, records contributions by different stakeholders to clarify ownership, and captures the full decision-making pathway. This makes the AI's contribution transparent and reviewable.

Conversational Flow

ADA's conversation follows a structured but non-prescriptive cycle. ADA makes suggestions and surfaces evidence, then participants discuss and reply. ADA reviews and seeks confirmation before moving forward. This iterative loop ensures human decision-making drives the process at every stage, while ADA provides structure and evidence to support those decisions. The conversation moves organically across phases, and ADA does not announce which phase it is in unless asked.

ADA's Evolution

ADA went through several iterations. The team began with a prescriptive blueprint prompt, focused on embedding the collaborative decision map and knowledge base. Testing revealed the need for a more conversational approach that facilitates genuine dialogue. The prompt was then revised into a modular architecture where each block serves a distinct purpose and can be adapted independently. The current version is a combined approach that balances structure with flexibility.

Evaluation: A Two-Layer Comparative Analysis

To evaluate ADA, the project compares two configurations across five assessment design scenarios (64 conversational turns). ADA A operates with the Collaborative Assessment Design Decision Making Framework as part of its knowledge base. ADA B operates without it. This paired design allows the team to isolate the effect of the structured flowchart on ADA's facilitation behaviour.

The analysis pipeline has two layers.

Layer 1. Surface-level comparison

This layer establishes a divergence baseline by measuring the overall similarity between ADA A and ADA B responses. Three metrics are used. Cosine similarity (Sentence-BERT, all-MiniLM-L6-v2) measures how semantically similar the full responses are at each turn. BERTScore (roberta-large) provides precision, recall, and F1 at the turn level. Hedge density, measured through six regex-based categories, captures the level of hedging language in each response.

Layer 2. Sentence-level analysis

This layer examines the content and structure of ADA's facilitation in finer detail. It has two components.

2a. Decision space classification

Using directed content analysis (Hsieh & Shannon, 2005), each sentence is classified into one of four decision spaces drawn from the Collaborative Assessment Design Decision Making Framework: Foundation and Purposes, Design and Alignment, Validating for VFI, and Design and Build. This reveals how each ADA configuration distributes its attention across the phases of assessment design, and how many transitions between phases occur per scenario.

2b. Dialogue act classification

Drawing on Stolcke et al. (2000), each sentence is classified into one of nine facilitation acts: SCAFFOLD, VALIDATE, SUMMARISE, RECOMMEND, PROBE, REFRAME, INFORM, DIRECT, and ELABORATE. Classification uses priority-ordered regex pattern matching in a first pass, followed by a carry-forward pass where ELABORATE sentences inherit the preceding act when appropriate. This reveals the facilitation profile of each ADA configuration and where they differ most.

Safeguarding: Edge Case Testing

Alongside the comparative analysis, the team tests ADA's responses to edge cases and unethical requests. This includes prompts susceptible to generating biased content and deliberately biased requests to verify that ADA responds appropriately and maintains its ethical guardrails.
