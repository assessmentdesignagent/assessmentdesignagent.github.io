A QAA Collaborative Enhancement Project

ADA Adaptive AI Agent for Assessment Design

A trained, instructed AI agent designed to support educators and students in creating evidence-informed, collaborative, and context-sensitive assessment designs.

Developed at the Eden Centre for Education Enhancement, London School of Economics and Political Science.

About the Project

Assessment design in higher education is highly context-dependent, shaped by disciplinary conventions, intended learning outcomes, institutional norms, and the characteristics of the student body. It is also inherently social and dialogic, requiring input from those who teach and those who learn. Yet it is frequently constrained by tight academic cycles, inherited practices, and a lack of structured support.

ADA was created in response to this context. Rather than imposing a solution from outside, ADA is designed to enter the existing activity system of assessment design and transform practice from within it. The project draws on a Vygotskian understanding of human activity, where context is constituted through the enactment of an activity involving people and artefacts.

What ADA Does

ADA is a trained, instructed Claude Project, currently running on the Haiku 4.5 model, built specifically for collaborative assessment design. Its prompt is structured around a six-block modular architecture, grounded in institutional policies, assessment principles, and empirical evidence. It is not a general-purpose chatbot.

Every element of its design reflects the project's design conjectures and pedagogical commitments, from how it opens a conversation, to how it surfaces evidence, to how it pauses for human discernment. ADA facilitates a human-centred process, focusing on how generative AI can support collaborative, context-sensitive, and evidence-informed assessment and feedback design.

Three Design Conjectures

The design and development of ADA is grounded in three high-level design conjectures about what assessment design needs to be.

1

Evidence-informed and context-sensitive

High-quality assessment design requires explicit engagement with research evidence, institutional policies, and discipline-specific norms, rather than relying on tacit knowledge or inherited practice.

2

A social decision-making process

Assessment design should involve appropriate stakeholders, including educators and students, developing richer assessment literacy through structured collaboration rather than isolated decision-making.

3

Timely and output-driven

Rather than working through abstract principles, course conveners should be guided toward reviewable outputs that make their thinking visible and their progress concrete.

From CARA to ADA

ADA was previously known as CARA (Collaborative Assessment Redesign AI assistant). Through iterative adoption, the project team identified that responsible and effective use requires making the agent's design transparent to both academic and student communities. Users should be enabled to critically evaluate and adjust the agent's instructions and knowledge base to suit their own contexts.

This prompted a re-conceptualisation. Rather than positioning course leaders and students as passive users of the agent, they should be supported as active makers who adapt the agent to their individual and collaborative contexts. The rename to ADA (Adaptive AI Agent for Assessment Design) reflects this capacity for user-driven adaptation.
