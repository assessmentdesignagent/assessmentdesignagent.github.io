User Guide

How to prepare for and work effectively with ADA

Before You Start

ADA works best when users come prepared with context about their course and assessment challenges. You do not need a finished plan. Even a one-line description of what you want to achieve is a good starting point. The more context you provide, the more targeted ADA’s facilitation will be.

1

Know your course context

What level is the course (UG, PG)? What discipline? How many students? What are the intended learning outcomes? What assessment methods are currently used?

2

Identify your assessment challenge

What is the specific problem or opportunity? Are you redesigning an existing assessment, creating something new, or responding to a specific concern such as GenAI use, group work, or marking criteria clarity?

3

Gather relevant documents

If you have existing assessment briefs, marking criteria, syllabus documents, or feedback templates, upload them to your project. Tell ADA if you want it to refer to specific documents in its responses.

4

Decide who is in the room

ADA is designed for collaborative use. If you are working with a student partner, an academic developer, or a co-marker, identify yourselves when you respond (for example, “Educator: ...” or “Student: ...”). ADA will adapt its facilitation accordingly.

Tips for Working with ADA

Prompt intuitively

If ADA gives you too much information, let it know. If you want it to focus on a specific area, say so. ADA is designed to follow your lead. For inspiration on how to structure your prompts, see the Suggested Prompts page.

Be intentional, not passive

ADA works best when you engage step by step. A one-liner asking it to do all the work will produce something generic. Provide context, respond to its questions, and push back when its suggestions don’t fit your situation.

Ask for pros and cons

At each decision point, ask ADA for arguments for and against different options. This surfaces considerations you might not have thought of and helps you make more deliberate choices.

Tell ADA when you’re ready to move on

When you’ve settled on a direction, tell ADA you’re ready for the next step or for an implementation plan. ADA won’t rush you, but it also won’t move forward until you signal that you’re satisfied.

What ADA Should Not Do

Understanding ADA’s boundaries is part of using it effectively. ADA is designed with explicit constraints.

ADA should not initiate assessment designs without first seeking contextual information about course requirements and stakeholder needs. It should not generate outputs like assessment briefs or marking criteria as final artefacts without prompting human review. It should not undermine the development of educator and student assessment and feedback literacy. The human is always in the driving seat.
