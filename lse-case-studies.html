Case Studies

Real-world examples of ADA in assessment design

Case Study: Financial Statement Group Project

The first live use of ADA (then known as CARA) involved redesigning the marking criteria for a financial accounting group project at LSE. The course leader, Dr Saipriya Kamath, was concerned that students were over-relying on AI tools to complete their financial analysis, potentially bypassing the learning process entirely.

The challenge

The group project required students to analyse financial statements of a company over 3 to 5 years around significant events such as COVID-19. The course leader noticed that while students were submitting polished reports, their performance on in-person exam questions requiring the same analytical skills had deteriorated in recent years. This suggested that the reports were being outsourced to AI rather than reflecting genuine learning.

The traditional process

"It usually takes me several days to design... The question is relatively easy because I know my subject. The hardest part was actually coming up with a marking criteria. Marking criteria for a quantitative question is relatively easy, but when you have something like a group project that has a report, the marking criteria takes a while to come up with." Dr Saipriya Kamath, Department of Accounting, LSE

Working with ADA

Dr Kamath came to the session with a general idea, roughly a one-liner, of what she wanted to achieve. Through structured conversation with ADA, facilitated by an academic developer, the session progressed through several key decision points.

1

Exploring implementation options

ADA proposed four approaches for incorporating AI baseline expectations into marking criteria. After discussion between the course leader and academic developer, they chose to add a dedicated AI Baseline Comparison criterion alongside an evidence trail requirement.

2

Defining how students demonstrate independent analysis

The pair chose critical comparison with AI outputs. Students would identify what the AI missed, got wrong, or oversimplified, then demonstrate how their own analysis addressed these shortcomings.

3

Addressing fairness

The academic developer flagged that requiring AI comparison assumed all students would use GenAI. They agreed that students who chose not to use AI would be provided with an exemplar AI analysis upon request.

4

Resolving the originality question

ADA's structured questioning helped the course leader realise that "originality" and "creativity" were problematic terms in financial analysis. After exploring arguments for and against keeping a separate creativity criterion, she decided to remove it entirely. The AI comparison criterion already captured the analytical sophistication that mattered.

5

Determining weighting

ADA presented arguments for equal weighting versus higher AI comparison weighting. The course leader's pedagogical goal, ensuring students actually learn financial analysis, led to a balanced structure with Content & Knowledge (25%), AI Baseline Comparison (25%), Research Skills (20%), Analytical Workings (20%), and Organisation (10%).

The outcome

"It was surprisingly good. I came with a very general idea... and I was able to have a conversation with the agent. The agent gave interesting directions to take, but also asked some piercing questions which made me think of how exactly I wanted to proceed. The process only took about an hour and a half." Dr Saipriya Kamath

The session produced a complete, revised marking rubric with performance descriptors across five bands, an accommodation for students who prefer not to use AI, explicit requirements for analytical workings, and a clear decision pathway documenting how each design choice was made and why.

"The agent just accelerates everything and it does bring new perspectives, again, with the right prompts. The prompts are the key to it... Being very intentional about the prompts and then trying to get different perspectives, asking the AI for pros and cons at each step, you end up with a very good assessment criteria." Dr Saipriya Kamath

Wider Adoption at LSE

Beyond this initial case, ADA has been applied in several contexts at LSE, including adoption by individual users across departments, by the LSE school-wide oral assessment working group, and by the LSE PGCertHE cohort. These applications continue to inform the iterative development of ADA's modular architecture.
